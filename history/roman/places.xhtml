Tacitus, Annals 12.50:

On the advance of the Parthians, the Iberians dispersed without a battle, and the Armenian cities, Artaxata and Tigranocerta, submitted to the yoke.
  • Artaxata is a city in Armenia.
  • Tigranocerta is a city in Armenia.

Tacitus, Annals 15.1:

Tigranes, quitting Armenia, had ravaged the Adiabeni, a people on its border, ...
  • Armenia borders Adiabene.

Tacitus, Annals 15.4:

He had, in fact, occupied Tigranocerta, a city strong from the multitude of its defenders and the vastness of its fortifications. In addition, the river Nicephorius, the breadth of which is far from contemptible, circled a portion of its walls, and a wide fosse was drawn where they distrusted the protection of the stream.
  • Tigranocerta is on the river Nicephorius.

Tacitus, Annals 15.5:

... at the town Nisibis, thirty-seven miles distant from Tigranocerta ...
  • Nisibis is near Tigranocerta.

If Tigranocerta is a city in Armenia, and Nisibis is near Tigranocerta, then Nisibis is a town in Armenia.

Tacitus, Annals 15.12:

Corbulo, perfectly fearless, left half his army in Syria to retain the forts built on the Euphrates, and taking the nearest route, which also was not deficient in supplies, marched through the country of Commagene, then through Cappadocia, and thence into Armenia.
  • The river Euphrates flows through Syria.
  • Syria borders Commagene.
  • Commagene borders Cappadocia.
  • Cappadocia borders Armenia.

Tacitus, Annals 2.56:

This had been of old an unsettled country from the character of its people and from its geographical position, bordering, as it does, to a great extent on our provinces and stretching far away to Media.
  • "This" refers to Armenia, so Armenia borders Media.

Tacitus, Annals 3.45:

Silius meantime was advancing with two legions, and having sent forward some auxiliary troops was ravaging those villages of the Sequani, which, situated on the border, adjoin the Aedui, and were associated with them in arms.
  • The territory of the Sequani borders the territory of the Aedui.

Tacitus, Annals 2.53:

Germanicus, however, entered on the office at Nicopolis, a city of Achaia, whither he had arrived by the coast of Illyricum, after having seen his brother Drusus, who was then in Dalmatia, and endured a stormy voyage through the Adriatic and afterwards the Ionian Sea.
  • Nicopolis is a city in Achaia.
  • The Adriatic Sea borders the Ionian Sea.
  • Dalmatia has a coast on the Adriatic Sea.
  • Achaia has a coast on the Ionian Sea.
  • Dalmatia is in Illyricum.
... and, at the same time, in remembrance of his ancestors, he visited the bay which the victory of Actium had made famous, ...
  • Actium is near Nicopolis.
Thence he went to Athens, ...
  • Germanicus is in Athens.

Tacitus, Annals 2.54:

Thence he directed his course to Euboea and crossed to Lesbos, ... He then penetrated to the remoter parts of the province of Asia, visited the Thracian cities, Perinthus and Byzantium; next, the narrow strait of the the Propontis and the entrance of the Pontus, ...
  • Perinthus is a city in Thrace.
  • Byzantium is a city in Thrace.
  • Euboea is en route from Athens to Lesbos.
  • Perinthus is en route from Lesbos to Byzantium.
  • The Propontis borders the Pontus.

"the Pontus" refers to Pontus Euxinus (Black Sea).

In his return he attempted to see the sacred mysteries of the Samothracians, but north winds which he encountered drove him aside from his course. And so after visiting Ilium and surveying a scene venerable from the vicissitudes of fortune and as the birth-place of our people, he coasted back along Asia, and touched at Colophon, to consult the oracle of the Clarian Apollo.
  • Ilium is en route from Byzantium to Colophon.
  • The oracle of the Clarian Apollo is at Colophon.
  • Germanicus is in Colophon.
  • Samothrace is an island in the Aegean Sea.

Tacitus, Annals 2.55:

Cneius Piso meanwhile, that he might the sooner enter on his design, terrified the citizens of Athens by his tumultuous approach, ...
  • Cneius Piso is in Athens.
Then, by sailing rapidly and by the shortest route through the Cyclades, he overtook Germanicus at the island of Rhodes.
  • The Cyclades are en route from Athens to Rhodes
  • Cneius Piso and Germanicus are at Rhodes.
Scarcely allowing a day's interval, he left Germanicus and hastened on in advance. When he reached Syria ...
  • Rhodes is en route from the Cyclades to Syria
  • Cneius Piso is in Syria.
Of all this Germanicus was aware, but his most pressing anxiety was to be first in reaching Armenia.
  • Rhodes is en route from Colophon to Armenia
  • Germanicus is in Armenia.

Tacitus, Annals 2.57:

At length the two met at Cyrrhus, the winter-quarters of the tenth legion, each controlling his looks, Piso concealing his fears, Germanicus shunning the semblance of menace.
  • The winter-quarters of the tenth legion are at Cyrrhus.
  • Cyrrhus is in Syria.
  • Cneius Piso and Germanicus are at Cyrrhus.

Tacitus, Annals 2.58:

He begged meanwhile that Vonones might not be kept in Syria, where; by emissaries from an easy distance, he might draw the chiefs of the tribes into civil strife. Germanicus' answer as to the alliance between Rome and Parthia was dignified: as to the king's visit and the respect shown to himself, it was graceful and modest. Vonones was removed to Pompeiopolis, a city on the coast of Cilicia.
  • Pompeiopolis is a city on the coast of Cilicia.
  • Germanicus removes Vonones from Syria to Pompeiopolis.

"Vonones" refers to Vonones I.
